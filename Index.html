Overview of Social Media

  • Social media are forms of digital communication.
  • Early platforms (e.g., MySpace and LiveJournal) were designed for people to connect with friends and family.
  • Social media use grew rapidly in the 2000s and early 2010s.
  • Initially used for socializing among college-age users.
  • Different platform purposes.
  • Social media became commercialized.

Pros

  • Connectivity & community-building
  • Information dissemination
  • Educational resource
  • Entertainment

Cons

  • Addiction
  • Dis/misinformation
  • Cyberbullying / harassment
  • Privacy concerns

Social Media Addiction

  • Social media addiction is a behavioral addiction.
  • Social media addiction is characterized by excessive and compulsive usage that affects daily functioning and overall well-being.

Signs of social media addiction:

  • Mood modification
  • Salience
  • Tolerance
  • Withdrawal symptoms
  • Conflict
  • Relapse

Effect – Increasing Mental Illness

Social media addiction can lead to:

  • Increased anxiety
  • Increased depression
  • Low self-esteem
  • Emotional dysregulation
  • High stress levels
  • Decreased self control

A review of multiple studies regarding social media addiction and college students found that acts of psychological distress such as symptoms of anxiety and depression are shown to be both a cause and effect of heavy social media use.

  • All 22 studies reviewed support a link between social media addiction and an increase in mental illnesses.

Effect - Increasing Addictive Behaviors

Key Facts:

  • 210 million people around the globe are addicted to social media.
  • Social media addiction is more prevalent in younger generations.
  • 82% of Gen Z adults have admitted to having a heavy reliance on social media.
  • Over 60% of college students in the U.S. report being addicted to social media.

Causes of Social Media Addiction:

  • Mental illness (anxiety, depression, etc.)
  • Low self-esteem
  • Desire for social acceptance among peers
  • Comparison
  • Peer pressure
  • Exposure to unrealistic online presence
  • Addictive algorithms
  • Endless scrolling features

Effect - Decreasing Academic Engagement

Social media addiction is not proven to directly link to decreasing academic engagement in college students, however research shows significant indirect and inverse effects on academic engagement overall.

  • Addiction to social media increases symptoms of depression and anxiety.
  • When mental-illness symptoms increase, academic engagement decreases.
  • Academic engagement positively correlates with self-esteem but has an inverse relationship with depression.

Our Stance on Social Media Addiction

Position

  • Social media is not inherently harmful, but many features are designed to maximize time on app.
  • Heavy, unplanned use relates to anxiety, sleep problems, and lower well-being for some users.
  • A minority show addiction-like patterns such as loss of control, withdrawal, and conflict.
  • Risks are higher for adolescents and students during stressful periods.

What We Support

  • Design transparency and default time-use tools that are easy to set and hard to ignore.
  • Digital literacy that teaches reward loops, algorithms, and mindful use.
  • Environment changes: notification hygiene, bedtime protection, study-first device setup.
  • Access to help when use causes distress or impairment.

What is being done about social media addiction?

  • Media Addicts Anonymous
    A peer support group focused on problematic social media use. Offers online meetings and a step-by-step approach for people who feel their habits are hard to change and want community while rebuilding healthier routines.
  • National Rehab Hotline: 866-210-3013
    24/7 free line for questions and referrals related to social media addiction, mental health, and other addictions. They’ll help you talk through next steps and connect you with local support if you want it.

What we’re doing about social media addiction?

  • Proposal: Introduce a club or support group that teaches and encourages healthier technology habits.
  • Programming: The club/support group will host events and meetings to raise awareness and reach out to those struggling with addiction. Events can also raise money to support students in the community who don’t have funds to see health advisors and counselors.
  • Campus support: Along with the new club, create a dedicated campus counseling phone line for social media addiction (in addition to existing resources).

Discussions and advice for those with social media addiction:

  • Set times/spaces to be tech-free
  • Detox periods
  • Find alternative activities
  • More in-person communication
  • Use technology to help (Focus, limits)
  • Seek professional help
